Adaptive Cortical Clustering and Multi-Band
Encoding for SEEG-to-Speech Synthesis

Woojin Chung and Hong-Goo Kang

School of Electrical and Electronic Engineering, Yonsei University

Abstract

Brain-to-speech synthesis offers a critical framework for restoring communication in individuals with severe speech impairments. However, current systems are constrained by a primary reliance on high-gamma bands and uniform neural channel processing that ignores functional specialization. These challenges are particularly pronounced in stereoelectroencephalography (sEEG), where depth electrodes traverse various cortical and subcortical layers, creating ambiguity in anatomical region assignment. Furthermore, such systems are highly susceptible to overfitting in data-scarce conditions. To address this, we propose a framework for sEEG-to-speech synthesis that integrates three complementary components: (1) an adaptive region clustering module to identify functionally coherent electrode groups based on inter-channel correlations; (2) a region-wise multi-band encoder to capture spectral information across frequency bands using learnable region-specific fusion; and (3) a Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) strategy to efficiently adapt pre-trained Transformers to sparse neural datasets. Evaluated on sEEG recordings from three participants, our framework achieves state-of-the-art mel-spectrogram reconstruction (Pearson correlation: 0.872), outperforming various state-of-the-art baselines. Further analyses reveal that speech encoding relies on distinct spectral channels across different regions. Our findings challenge the prevailing high-gamma-centric paradigm and underscore the necessity of region-wise multi-band integration strategies in clinical brain-computer interfaces.
